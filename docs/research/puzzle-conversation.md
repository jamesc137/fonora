# Can strangers recover meaning?

> **TL;DR.** This is the live edge of the notebook. Every prior experiment was building toward one test: put two people who share only the roots together, and see whether they can recover each other's intended meaning. The puzzle-conversation protocol and playtests are how we measure that — and the answer is still being written, which is why this note is *Open*.

## The question

Does the whole system pass its own test? Can two root-knowers — with no shared native language — recover each other's intended meaning from compounds neither of them rehearsed?

## The hypothesis

Recoverable meaning is measurable through *play*. A structured **puzzle conversation** — one person builds a meaning-attempt, another guesses it — plus recorded playtests reveal communicative success better than any automated score. Heuristics rank candidate compounds; humans decide whether they actually land.

## The constraints

- Automated scores **rank only**; human guess-the-meaning playtests are the verdict.
- The protocol exercises the **communicative core** (the ~50 roots the experiment is really about).
- Results feed back into the dictionary's preferred and alternate forms.

## What we built

The [Puzzle Conversation](/language#puzzle) tool and a playtests data file ([`data/fonoran-playtests.json`](https://github.com/jamesc137/fonora/blob/main/data/fonoran-playtests.json)), introduced when the project reoriented around the communication experiment. The judging philosophy is set out in the [Constitution](../fonoran-constitution.md).

## What happened

This is the current frontier rather than a closed result. Reading, writing, translating, and typing are in place; the open question is empirical — across real playtests, how often does recovery succeed, and what makes a compound recoverable versus opaque?

## The question that followed

This note is the live end of the thread. The next research notes will branch from here: what the playtests reveal about which roots and compounds communicate, and how the tiered core should grow in response.
