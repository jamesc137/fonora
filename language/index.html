Fonoran

Communicate by constructing meaning, not memorizing it.

Fonoran is an experimental language built from a small set of shared semantic roots. Instead of memorizing thousands of words, speakers combine concepts to create new meaning.

Our research asks a simple question:

Can two people with no shared native language learn enough of this system to communicate after only a short period of study?

A Tiered Language

Fonoran is not one flat vocabulary. It grows in three rings. Each ring adds reach while keeping the same compositional approach of combining shared roots.

  1. 1

    Communicative Core

    Roughly 50 roots

    The minimum for communication

    The vocabulary needed for everyday survival and social ideas — the set the experiment actually tests. The question is not “can people learn 500 roots?” but how much two strangers can communicate with only fifty.

    Every proposed root must pass the Campfire Test:

    Could this realistically come up between two strangers with no shared language during their first week together?

  2. 2

    Extended Core

    Roughly 100 roots

    Everyday fluency

    Expands communication into ordinary conversation — work, weather, family, routine needs — while preserving the same root-combination strategy. Still gated by recoverable meaning, not linguistic completeness.

  3. 3

    Complete Language

    Specialized and abstract vocabulary

    An open-ended layer for technical, scientific, and abstract concepts. These roots are admitted only when simpler combinations repeatedly prove insufficient for the ideas speakers actually need.

Ordered by human experience

Within each tier, roots are grouped the way a person encounters the world — not by linguistic category. A stranded pair needs hungry, hurt, water before transfer or pulse. Bodily and spatial concepts come before social ones; emotion and time follow; thinking and abstraction enter last.

This ordering keeps the early vocabulary aligned with what strangers would plausibly need to coordinate. Abstract concepts are added only when simpler combinations consistently fail in practice.

  1. survival eat, drink, sleep, danger, help
  2. body hand, eye, pain, sick, hot, cold
  3. space up, down, inside, near, far
  4. objects water, fire, tree, animal, path
  5. people friend, family, group, leader
  6. actions give, take, work, share, move
  7. emotions happy, afraid, angry, trust, hope
  8. time now, before, after, tomorrow
  9. thinking know, remember, understand, question
  10. abstraction cause, change, value — only when simpler roots fail
